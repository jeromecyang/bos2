July Fourth Celebration: Camping at Delaware Water Gap

I went camping with my previous roommate Bo over the weekend. Since I wasn't planning to take any extra day off, a 3-day weekend doesn't allow us to go far. Delaware Water Gap National Recreational Area was picked simply because of my loyalty to the national park service (NPS) - I believe most NPS units would not disapoint me. And also because its closeness to Boston, which ensured that we wouldn't spend too much time moving.

The drive from Boston to Stokes State Forest (where we camped) took roughly 4 hours. The area itself, is a long valley, which can take about an hour to drive through (one way). Both riverbanks are rich in natural landscapes and historic landmarks. Certain attractions were super packed during mid-day, but overall it wasn't overwhelming.

July 1, 2016

Next MBTA Subway?

Like its counterparts in New York and Philadelphia, the MBTA subways is one of the most historic rapid transit systems in this country, and has not added any new line for decades. What if now the city decides to build a new line? How should this line go?

I think this line needs to meet the follow purposes:

  • Providing underserved neighborhoods an easy access to downtown.
  • Intersecting as many lines as possible, to make transfer easier.
  • Replacing popular bus routes along busier corridors.

The new subway line I would draw, the yellow line, would serve neighborhoods that have no subway access currently. on the north, they would be Charleston, Chelsea, Revere and Saugus; on the south, they would be South End, Roxbury, Mattanpan (connecting with current red line) and Hyde Park. Interestingly, those neighborhoods used to have rails or trolleys until buses and cars became the primary means of travel. Bringing rail/subway transit back to those places would not only provide better accessibility, but also restore many historic transit corridors, which could eventually make the city greener and more transit-friendly.
